Russian Orthodox Church — Moscow Patriarchate

Official Statement of the Russian Orthodox Church

Released simultaneously in Russian, English, Greek, and Serbian
Office of the Patriarch of Moscow and All Russia

The Holy Synod of the Russian Orthodox Church has convened in extraordinary session to address the material recently released by the platform known as RawHistory.wtf, identified in media coverage as Release Two, depicting the figure of Grigori Efimovich Rasputin. We issue this statement with full awareness that whatever we say will be insufficient. We issue it anyway because silence is not available to us.

On the Question of Authenticity

The Holy Synod has consulted with technical advisors, historians of the relevant period, and scholars of Orthodox material culture. We are not in a position to declare the footage fraudulent. We wish we were. We state this plainly because we believe the faithful deserve plainness on a matter of this gravity. Our advisors have examined what is visible of the interior — the iconostasis arrangement, the specific saints represented, the manner of their illumination — and have found nothing that allows us to dismiss what we are looking at as fabrication.

The icons in that room are correct. We do not elaborate on why the correctness of the icons is significant. Those who understand Orthodox practice will understand. Those who do not will find adequate explanation in the commentary that has accompanied this release. We note only that whoever filmed that room either had access to records of extraordinary specificity or filmed the actual room, and that the former explanation has not been demonstrated by anyone to our satisfaction.

On the Figure of Rasputin

Grigori Rasputin was never canonized. He was never formally affiliated with the Russian Orthodox Church in any recognized capacity. He presented himself as a man of God. The Church's historical position is that this presentation was at minimum complicated and at maximum fraudulent. We are reviewing that position. Not because the footage vindicates him. We want to be precise here. The footage does not vindicate Rasputin. What the footage depicts is not consistent with Orthodox teaching, with Orthodox practice, or with Orthodox morality. Nothing in this statement should be read as suggesting otherwise.

What the footage depicts is a man who believed. This is the difficulty we are sitting with and will continue to sit with and cannot resolve in a single statement or perhaps in a single generation of theological reflection. The man in that footage is not performing faith as a manipulation. He is not using the sacred as a costume. The sacred is present in that room in a way that our advisors — men and women who have spent their lives in the study and practice of Orthodox spirituality — cannot dismiss. What he did with it is another matter. What he believed about what he did with it is the matter that will occupy us for some time.

On the Khlysty Question

The Church has historically and officially rejected any association between Rasputin and the Khlysty sect, whose practices — including the theology of sin-as-prerequisite-for-repentance — are condemned as heresy. We are not withdrawing that condemnation. We are acknowledging that the footage raises questions about the simple version of our historical position that we cannot answer quickly or cleanly. The relationship between what is visible in that footage and what the Khlysty allegedly practiced is a matter now requiring serious theological examination rather than categorical dismissal. We did not expect to be writing that sentence. We are writing it because intellectual honesty before God requires it.

On the Platform Itself

The Russian Orthodox Church calls upon the operators of RawHistory.wtf to cease distribution of this material immediately. We are aware this call is unlikely to be heeded. We make it because our pastoral responsibility requires it and because the record should show that we made it.

We note with concern the effect this footage has demonstrably had on the faithful. We have received communication from parishes across Russia, Eastern Europe, and the broader Orthodox diaspora describing responses to this material that range from spiritual crisis to something our priests are struggling to categorize. We are directing all clergy to make themselves available for confession and counsel without restriction in the coming weeks.

We note also — and this is the sentence this statement has been building toward and which the Holy Synod debated for four hours before including — that the platform has now twice demonstrated an ability to show us the past as it actually was, unmediated, uninterpreted, without the distance that history normally provides. The theological implications of that ability are not something we can address in a press statement. They are something we must address. We will address them. It will take time. It will require scholarship and prayer and a willingness to sit with questions that do not resolve. The Church has sat with unresolvable questions before. We are sitting with them again.

On What We Ask of the Faithful

We ask for prayer. We ask for patience. We ask that those who have viewed this material bring what it has stirred in them to their confessor and to God rather than to internet forums at two in the morning. We ask this knowing it is probably too late for most of them. We ask it anyway.

This statement was approved by the Holy Synod of the Russian Orthodox Church following four hours of deliberation. Three members of the Synod dissented from the inclusion of the fifth section. Their dissent is noted and respected. The statement stands as written.

The Patriarch of Moscow and All Russia did not issue a personal statement. He has not responded to requests for comment. He is, according to his office, in prayer.
