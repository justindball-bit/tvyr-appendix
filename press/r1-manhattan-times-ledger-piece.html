The Manhattan Times-Ledger

"What Copyright? He Doesn't Even Have A Face"

On West 46th Street, a novelty store owner is making a killing off history's hottest new scandal. He has no regrets. He has, however, run out of mugs.
By Patricia Kwan, Staff Reporter

Rocky Deluca has been in the novelty business for nineteen years. He has sold hats that say things that cannot be repeated in a family publication. He has sold foam fingers at sizes and in configurations that stretch the definition of foam finger. He has survived Times Square's sanitisation, two recessions, a pandemic, and the specific economic brutality of a landlord who he describes, with remarkable consistency across the entire interview, as "that guy." He has never, he tells me, had a week like this one. "I'm not gonna lie to you," he says, gesturing at the organised chaos of Rocco's Novelty Emporium, West 46th Street, Manhattan. "I saw the BBC thing Tuesday morning, I watched the clip, and before it finished I was already on the phone with my guy in Queens." His guy in Queens does the printing. "I said Tommy, clear the schedule. We got a situation."

The store is, at 11 AM on a Thursday, doing the kind of business Rocky normally associates with the week before Christmas. The TimelessFlesh display occupies what was previously the spinner rack for celebrity caricature magnets. It is comprehensive. It is enthusiastic. It has, in several respects, gone further than most licensed merchandise operations would consider advisable.

The Flagship Item

The flagship item is a ceramic mug. Julius Caesar on one side, Cleopatra on the other. Cleopatra is, when the mug is empty, wearing a white dress rendered in heat-sensitive ink. "You put a hot beverage in," Rocky explains, with the satisfied expression of a man describing his masterwork, "the dress disappears. She's still tasteful," he adds quickly. "We kept it tasteful. It's historically suggestive. That's how I describe it. Historically suggestive." The mugs are $24.99. He has sold four hundred and twelve of them since Wednesday morning. "I reordered twice already," he says. "Tommy thinks I'm insane. Tommy is currently buying a boat with what I've paid him this week, so."

The shirt range is extensive. There is a classic caesar portrait tee with the text THE ORIGINAL PLAYER beneath it. There is a Cleopatra silhouette shirt reading SHE KNEW WHAT SHE WAS DOING. There is a couples shirt set — Caesar on one, Cleopatra on the other — that Rocky describes as his "relationship goals" line, which he delivers without irony. There is a shirt that simply reads TIMELESSFLESH.XXX in the site's font, which Rocky acknowledges exists in a legal grey area he has chosen not to examine. "Is that their font exactly?" I ask. "It's a font," he says carefully. "There are many fonts."

Historically Protected

The condoms are displayed behind the counter in a small rotating stand that Rocky has labeled, in hand-drawn marker, HISTORICALLY PROTECTED. There are four varieties. Julius and Cleo — The Original Hookup. (Ribbed. "For historical accuracy," Rocky says, and will not elaborate.) Empire Builder. (Extra large. The packaging features a laurel wreath.) Nile Queen. ("That one's for the ladies," Rocky says. "My wife told me to make one for the ladies. She's been right about everything for twenty-two years so.") The Dark Tier. (Black packaging. No other description. "I don't explain that one," Rocky says. "People who know, know.") I ask who is buying the condoms. "Everyone," he says simply. "Young people, old people, yesterday a guy in a suit bought six boxes of Empire Builder and did not make eye contact with me once. Didn't need to. We understood each other."

The Copyright Question

I ask the question I have been building toward. Is Rocky at all concerned about copyright infringement? About the possibility that whoever owns TimelessFlesh.xxx might take legal action against a novelty store selling merchandise based on their content? Rocky looks at me for a moment. Then he does something between a laugh and a sigh, a sound particular to men who have been in small Manhattan businesses for a very long time and have developed a refined sense of which problems are real problems. "Copyright," he says. "Copyright," I confirm. "What copyright?" he says. "Julius Caesar has been dead for two thousand years. Cleopatra has been dead for two thousand years. Those are public domain faces. I can put Caesar on a mug, I can put him on a thong, I can put him on a birthday cake, nobody owns Julius Caesar. Julius Caesar is free. Julius Caesar is the most free guy there is. He doesn't even have a trademark." I note that the site's name and font might be protected. Rocky waves this away. "That's a font," he says, again. "There are many fonts."

I ask about the content itself — whether reproducing imagery associated with the footage might constitute infringement. "I'm not reproducing the footage," he says patiently. "I'm reproducing history. Caesar and Cleopatra were together. That's in every history book. I'm selling historically inspired merchandise." He picks up an Empire Builder. "This is inspired by the Roman Empire. The Roman Empire is not gonna sue me."

The Fig Condoms

I raise the more delicate point. Even if Rocky is correct about the historical figures themselves — and several intellectual property attorneys I spoke to afterward suggested the situation was more complicated than Rocky's analysis, though they agreed it was genuinely novel — what about the site owner? Rocky puts down the condom. He thinks about this with what appears to be genuine consideration. "Okay," he says. "Let's say you're right. Let's say there's some guy somewhere who has this ability, right, he can look back through time, he's running this whole operation, very sophisticated, very anonymous, doesn't talk to anybody, has his whole thing set up so nobody can find him." He spreads his hands. "That guy is going to come after me over flavored condoms?" I ask which flavor. "Fig," he says. "We went with fig. Tommy's idea. Romans ate figs. It's thematic." I ask again — does he think the site operator would pursue legal action? "I think," Rocky says carefully, "that a guy who is actively trying to stay invisible, who has set up his whole business so no government or law firm or anybody can locate him — I think that guy is not going to walk into a Manhattan courthouse to file a claim against Rocco's Novelty Emporium." He picks the condom back up. "I think that guy looks at me and sees free marketing." He puts it back on the stand. "Also I'm naming the fig ones after him. The Voyeur." He points at a small handwritten sign I had not previously noticed. "Limited edition. Signed by me. Thirty-nine ninety-nine." He has sold twenty-seven of them.

Multitudes

I ask Rocky if he's seen the footage. Something shifts slightly in his face. The salesman steps back for a moment and someone else is briefly there. "Yeah," he says. I ask what he thought. He's quiet for a second. Looks at the display. Looks back at me. "I thought — " he stops. Starts again. "I've been selling this stuff my whole life, right. Novelty. Joke gifts. Things that are funny for thirty seconds. That's the business." He picks up a Caesar mug, not the heat-sensitive one, just a regular one, turns it in his hands. "I watched that footage and I thought, that's not a novelty. That's — those are people. Those are just two people." He sets the mug down. "Then I thought, I can put her on a mug." He grins. It is a good grin. Completely unashamed. "I contain multitudes," he says. "My wife tells me that. She read it somewhere."

As I leave, Rocky calls after me. "Tell your readers," he says, "the mugs are going fast. I'm not restocking after this batch. Collector's items." I ask if that's true. "No," he says cheerfully. "Tommy's making five hundred more tonight. But it sounds good." Rocco's Novelty Emporium, West 46th Street, Manhattan. Cash preferred. Rocky does not have a website. "I'm not an internet guy," he says. "I'm a stuff guy." The Voyeur fig-flavored condoms are, as of press time, sold out. Rocky says he's not surprised. "People always want the thing they're not supposed to want," he says. "That's not a new observation. That's just Tuesday."
