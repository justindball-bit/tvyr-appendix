"I Have Spent Fifteen Years Defending Him"

Dr. Irina Volkov on RawHistory.wtf's second release, and what it cost her to watch it.
By Dr. Irina Volkov, Associate Professor of Russian History, Moscow State University

I published my first paper on Grigori Rasputin in 2009. It was titled, with the confidence of a thirty-one year old who had just completed her doctorate and believed that archives were the antidote to mythology, The Fabricated Devil: Political Slander and the Construction of Rasputin's Sexual Reputation, 1905-1916. The argument was straightforward. The stories — the orgies, the sect practices, the women of the court lined up outside his apartment on Gorokhovaya Street — were weapons. Manufactured by his enemies, amplified by a press that understood scandal, weaponized by aristocrats who resented a Siberian peasant with the Empress's ear. The man himself was complicated, morally imperfect, politically dangerous. But the specific mythology of Rasputin as some kind of dark sexual prophet — that, I argued, was fiction. Useful fiction. Fiction with a purpose.

I have spent fifteen years building that argument into a book, a course, a reputation.

I watched the footage on a Thursday evening in my apartment in Moscow.

I am going to tell you what I found. I owe that much to the field and to myself. I am going to be precise and I am going to be honest and if this ends my career as I have built it then that is the price of intellectual honesty and I will pay it.

What I Expected

I expected a fraud. I have followed the RawHistory.wtf releases with professional attention since the Caesar/Cleopatra footage broke in October. I authenticated nothing and disputed nothing publicly because I had no standing in that particular historical period and because the authentication question seemed adequately covered by colleagues with more relevant expertise. What I noted, professionally, was the editing. The restraint of it. Whoever was behind the operation understood that suggestion was more powerful than explicitness, that the two-second hold on Cleopatra's face was worth more than anything more direct would have been. I filed this as the mark of a sophisticated fraudster. Someone who understood not just technology but psychology. Someone who knew that the audience's imagination, properly directed, would do more work than any fabrication could.

I expected the Rasputin release to be the same. A sophisticated fabrication, more ambitious than the first, designed to confirm rather than challenge the mythology I had spent fifteen years dismantling. I expected to watch it and find the seams.

The Apartment

The apartment is correct.

I want to say that simply and let it sit there before I continue. The apartment on Gorokhovaya Street, as it appears in the footage, is correct. The layout is consistent with the floor plan documented in the Okhrana surveillance records I have worked with extensively. The specific placement of the icons — and there were many, Rasputin was genuinely, not performatively devout — matches descriptions in three separate contemporary accounts I have cross-referenced over the course of my career.

The icons themselves. I paused the footage and looked at them for a long time. No fabricator, however skilled, however well-resourced, builds a set that gets the icons right. Not because the information doesn't exist — it does, in archives most people don't know to look in — but because no fabricator thinks the icons matter. They are background. They are set dressing. They are not the thing anyone came to see. Whoever filmed this knew the icons mattered. They matter because they are the context for everything else in the footage. Because Rasputin did not separate the sacred from what he did in that room. That was the point. That was the theology, if theology is the right word for it — and I have spent fifteen years arguing it wasn't theology, it was slander dressed as theology. The icons are correct and they are lit the way they would be lit by the specific candles burning in front of them and the light they cast moves the way candlelight moves and I could not find the seam.

The Women

I am not going to describe them in detail. That is not what this piece is for and I do not have the stomach for it. I will say this. They came to him. That is the first thing. They were there before he arrived in the room — the footage begins with them already present, already waiting. Whatever brought them to that apartment on that night, they came willingly. More than willingly. The specific quality of their anticipation is something I am still trying to find clinical language for and failing.

I will say that one of them was young. Not a child — I want to be very clear about that — but young in the way of women who have not yet learned to want things quietly. I will say that the other was older, and that she watched Rasputin with an expression I have seen in religious iconography but not on a living face, and that this disturbed me more than anything else in the footage. I will say that neither of them looked like victims. I have spent fifteen years arguing that the women in the Rasputin mythology were victims of a narrative that reduced their agency to weakness and their faith to manipulation. I still believe that. I believe it more carefully now.

Rasputin

I have looked at more photographs of Grigori Rasputin than most people look at photographs of their own families. I know his face the way you know a face you have studied across fifteen years of professional obsession. The footage confirmed the photographs in the way the Caesar footage confirmed the busts — the architecture was recognisable, but the weather of the face was something the photographs could not have prepared me for.

He was completely still. That is the first thing. In a room that was — I will not describe it further — in a room where stillness would not be most people's natural state, he was completely still. Not the stillness of a man suppressing something. The stillness of a man for whom this was entirely, cosmically ordinary. The stillness of a man who believed, in the most complete sense of that word, that what he was doing was sanctioned. By God. By the universe. By whatever force he had decided looked through his eyes.

I have argued for fifteen years that the mysticism was performance. Peasant cunning dressed as prophecy. A provincial man who had identified a gap in the market — the spiritual hunger of aristocratic women in a court that was rotting from the inside — and filled it with carefully constructed charisma. The man in that footage was not performing. That is not a sentence I expected to write. I have written it four times in four different ways and this is the only version that is true. He was not performing. Whatever he was doing in that room, he believed in it entirely. And the women believed in him entirely. And I watched it — I watched all six minutes and forty seconds of it — and I understood, in a way that fifteen years of archival research had not prepared me to understand, why they came to him. I understood it and I cannot unknow it and my book goes to press in four months and I do not know what I am going to do about that.

What I Am Left With

The mythology was not entirely wrong. That is the conclusion I am left with. The mythology was not entirely wrong, which means my argument was not entirely right, which means fifteen years of work has not been wasted but has been — revised. Complicated. Made more true by being made less certain. I do not know if that is better or worse.

The Khlysty allegations — the sin-to-repent theology, the idea that spiritual ecstasy and physical ecstasy were not separate but continuous — I argued that this was slander. I still believe the political use of it was slander. I still believe his enemies took something real and made it a weapon. But something real. That is what the footage has given me and taken from me simultaneously. Something real. A man who was genuinely, completely, entirely what the mythology said he was, and who was also — and this is the thing I keep returning to, the thing I cannot file anywhere in my existing framework — at peace with it. Joyful, almost. In the specific way of someone who has resolved a question that torments most people and found, on the other side of the resolution, not shame but freedom.

I watched him and I thought: he has decided something. About what God is and what God permits and what the body is for. And he decided it so completely that there is no conflict left in him. None. I find that more disturbing than anything else in the footage. More disturbing than what the women were doing. More disturbing than the icons on the wall. A man with no conflict left in him.
