BBC World Service — Compiled by correspondents

Public Reaction: TimelessFlesh.xxx

Three continents. Same forty-eight hours.
Vox Pops — Field Compilation

Tuscaloosa, Alabama

Randy Pittman, 44, is standing outside a Sunoco station eating a gas station sandwich. He has opinions. "Look, I'm not saying I watched it. I'm not saying that. What I'm saying is my buddy Dale watched it and described it to me in considerable detail over the phone for about forty minutes, so I feel like I have a pretty good working knowledge of the situation." He takes a bite of the sandwich. "Here's my thing though. Here's my actual thing. Julius Caesar. Julius Caesar. That's the Roman guy, right? With the salad? And they're saying this is real. Like actually real. And I'm supposed to just go back to my Tuesday after that?" He gestures at nothing in particular. "Five hundred dollars a month I don't have. But whoever's charging it — I mean, respect. I genuinely respect the hustle. That's American, right there. That is a purely American move and I don't care what country it came from." He finishes the sandwich. "Dale's already subscribed. Dale makes bad decisions but I'm starting to think this one might work out for him."

Florence, Italy

Marco Feltrinelli, 67, retired professor of Renaissance history, is sitting at a table outside a café on the Via dei Tornabuoni. His espresso is untouched. He has been staring at his phone. He looks up slowly. "My first reaction — and I want to be precise about this — was professional. I looked at the light. I looked at the way the fabric moved. I looked at her hands. You can tell an enormous amount from hands in historical representation. How painters chose to render them. What they understood about gesture." He pauses. "Her hands were — " He stops again. He picks up the espresso. Puts it down. "I have spent forty years with the dead. You understand? Forty years trying to hear them through documents, through objects, through the things they left behind because leaving things behind was the only transmission available to them. And now someone — we don't know who, we don't know how — someone has simply looked." He is quiet for a moment. "The price is vulgar. The packaging is vulgar. The dot-xxx is — " he makes a gesture that encompasses all vulgarity and dismisses it. "Vulgar. And yet." He finally drinks the espresso. "I have already transferred the ten thousand dollars. Full archive access. I am not proud of this. I would do it again immediately."

Cairo, Egypt

Khaled Mansour, 31, is taking a break from his fruit cart near the Tahrir Square metro entrance. His cousin is minding the cart. He has watched the clip seven times on his phone, which he holds up as evidence. "Okay so first — Cleopatra. Our Cleopatra. Not the Hollywood one, not the American one, ours. She's in this." He says something in Arabic to his cousin, who responds without looking up. "He says she was Greek. I know she was Greek. She's still ours." He pockets the phone. "The thing that people are not talking about enough is — she looks like she's having a good time. Right? Like she is completely in charge of that situation. Two thousand years ago, most powerful woman in the world, still completely in charge. I found that very — " he searches for the word. "Reassuring." His cousin says something. Khaled laughs. "He says it's haram. But he also watched it." A customer approaches the cart. He holds up one finger — one moment. "Five hundred dollars I don't have. But my cousin in London has a subscription and he's going to call me on Friday." He grins. "Family rates."

Three people. Three continents. Same forty-eight hours. The Voyeur, somewhere in his well-appointed room, does not know any of them exist. He knows exactly what they are. They are the market.
