BBC World Service — Compiled by correspondents

Public Reaction: RawHistory.wtf — Release Two

Nobody is laughing this time.
Vox Pops — Field Compilation

Oslo, Norway

Astrid Bergmann, 52, is a secondary school history teacher. She is sitting in her car in the school parking lot. She has been sitting in her car in the school parking lot for forty minutes. She agreed to speak to us through the window. She did not get out of the car. "I teach this period," she says. "The Romanovs, the dissolution, the First World War feeding into the Revolution. I've taught it for twenty-two years. Rasputin is three lessons. Primary sources, historiography, the mythology versus the record." She looks at her hands on the steering wheel. "I watched it Sunday night. I've been trying to work out what I'm going to do with those three lessons since then."

I ask what she means. "The mythology versus the record," she says again. "That's the framing. That's been the framing for twenty years of scholarship — here's what people said about him, here's what actually happened, here's why the distance between those two things matters." She is quiet for a moment. "The footage doesn't fit that framing. The footage suggests the distance was smaller than we thought. Which means twenty-two years of lessons need — " she stops. "Adjusting." I ask if she's going to show the footage to her students. She looks at me for the first time. "No," she says. I ask why. "Because I'm forty-eight hours in and I still haven't worked out what I think about it," she says. "And I'm the adult." She rolled up the window after that. She was still in the car when we left.

Lagos, Nigeria

Emeka Okafor, 34, runs a small printing business and is eating lunch outside his shop. He is direct in a way that makes the interview move faster than most. "I watched it," he says. "My friend sent it to me. I watched it and then I sat for a while and then I called my friend and I said why did you send me this. He said because you needed to see it. I said I did not need to see it. He said yes you did."

I ask what his friend meant. "My friend is a philosopher," Emeka says, without elaboration, as though this explains everything. "He thinks people need to see things that disturb them. He has opinions about comfort and what it does to people." He is quiet for a moment. "He's usually right. I hate that he's usually right." I ask what disturbed him specifically. He thinks about this seriously. "The women went there," he says. "That's the thing. Nobody brought them. Nobody convinced them. They made a decision and they went there and what you see on their faces is — it's not what you expect. You expect something that confirms what you already think about situations like that. It doesn't confirm it." He finishes his food. "My friend says that's the point. That the whole site is about showing you the thing underneath the story you tell yourself about the thing." He shakes his head slowly. "My friend needs to get a different hobby." I ask if he's subscribed. "My friend is subscribed," he says. "He tells me things. That's enough for me." A pause. "For now."

Charleston, South Carolina

Donna Mayfield, 44, works in hospital administration. She agreed to speak to us on her lunch break, outside, away from her colleagues. She was very clear about that — away from her colleagues. "I'm going to tell you something and I need you to understand I'm a practical person," she says immediately. "I'm not someone who — I don't have mystical experiences. I don't go in for that. I'm a Methodist, I go to church, I believe in God in a normal way, not an extreme way."

I tell her I understand. "I watched that footage," she says, "and there was a moment — maybe two minutes in, maybe less — where I felt something in the room. Not on the screen. In my room. In my living room in Charleston where I was sitting on my couch with a glass of wine." She looks at me steadily, daring me to react. "I felt something in the room with me." I ask what it felt like. "Like being watched," she says. "Which I know how that sounds. I know exactly how that sounds. I'm telling you anyway because you asked." She straightens. "I turned the footage off. I sat there for a while. Then I turned it back on and finished it." She pauses. "I don't know what that man was. I don't know if it was God or something else entirely. I know that whatever it was it came through the screen and sat in my living room for about forty minutes and I couldn't tell if I wanted it to leave." She went back inside before I could ask another question.

Osaka, Japan

Kenji Watanabe, 28, is a graphic designer. He wants to talk about the editing. "Whoever cuts this footage is making decisions that are — they're not neutral decisions. The Caesar release, the way it held on her face at the end, two seconds longer than you expected — that was a choice. This release, the way it ends exactly where it ends, that half second before the cut, the older woman's face turning — that's a choice." He leans forward. "That's not a voyeur. A voyeur just points and watches. This person is an editor. This person understands that what you withhold is more powerful than what you show and they are using that understanding deliberately and precisely every single time."

I ask what he thinks that means about whoever is running the site. He thinks about it. "It means they felt it," he says. "Whatever is in that footage, they felt it first. Before any of us. They sat in a room alone and they watched it and they felt it and then they made decisions about how to make us feel it too." He pauses. "That's not a business decision. You don't need to be that precise for a business decision. That's something else." I ask what. He shakes his head. "I don't know," he says. "But I think about it more than I think about the footage itself. And I think about the footage a lot."
