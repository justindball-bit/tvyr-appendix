Maxim

"I've Never Been Disrupted Before. It's Interesting."

Feras Antoon, owner of Pornhub, sits down with Maxim to discuss TimelessFlesh.xxx, the nature of desire, and what it feels like to watch your industry get reinvented by someone with an ability that should not exist.
By Daniel Crane

Feras Antoon is not a man who appears rattled. This is, by all accounts, characteristic. The co-owner and CEO of MindGeek, the Montreal-based company that owns Pornhub and a portfolio of platforms that collectively constitute something close to the infrastructure of an entire industry, is known in business circles as a man who processes information quickly, speaks carefully, and does not perform emotions he is not having. So when I ask him about TimelessFlesh.xxx and he is quiet for a long moment before answering, I note it. "It's a genuinely interesting business," he says finally. This is, I will come to understand, the highest compliment Feras Antoon gives anything.

We are in Montreal, in a conference room that is aggressively normal — the kind of room that exists to signal that whatever you expected, this is not that. No art on the walls. Good coffee. A window overlooking a street where nothing in particular is happening. Antoon is dressed simply. He looks like a man who has been awake since five and has done considerable thinking since then. I ask him when he first became aware of the site. "Tuesday morning," he says. "Someone on my team sent me the BBC piece. I watched the footage before I finished reading the article." He pauses. "Then I read the article."

The Operational Read

I ask for his first reaction. "My first reaction was operational," he says. "Before anything else. Before the questions about what it was or how it existed. I looked at the structure and I thought — this person understands scarcity. Genuinely understands it, not as a concept but as a tool." He leans forward slightly. "Our industry's fundamental challenge for twenty years has been abundance. Content is infinite. When content is infinite, price goes to zero. That's not a business problem, that's physics." He picks up his coffee. "This person has solved that. One release per month. Full stop. You cannot buy more than one release per month regardless of what you're willing to pay. That's not a content strategy. That's a completely different relationship with the audience." He sets the coffee down. "I found that interesting before I found anything else interesting."

Does It Threaten Pornhub?

The question I have been sent here to ask is the obvious one. I ask it. Does TimelessFlesh.xxx threaten Pornhub? He looks at me with the specific expression of a man who has been asked a question he has already thoroughly answered for himself and is deciding how much of that answer to share. "It doesn't compete with us," he says carefully. "It's not in the same category. What we provide is — " he pauses — "volume. Variety. Accessibility. A different value proposition entirely." Another pause. "What this site provides is something I don't have a business category for yet." I ask what he means. "We have always operated on the principle that desire is universal and therefore the market is universal. That's true. That remains true." He folds his hands on the table. "But what this site is offering isn't just content. It's — " he looks at the window for a moment — "authentication. The thing itself. Not a representation, not a performance, not an approximation. The actual thing." He looks back at me. "I cannot compete with the actual thing. Nobody can compete with the actual thing. That's not a competitive landscape. That's a different universe."

I ask about the ten thousand dollar full access tier. Something changes in his expression. Not much. Enough. "In our industry there are metrics. Cost per acquisition. Lifetime customer value. Conversion rates at various price points. We have twenty years of data on what people will pay for content and at what threshold the price becomes prohibitive." He pauses. "Ten thousand dollars should be prohibitive. By every model we have, ten thousand dollars is a number that ends the conversation." I ask if it did. "They closed membership due to capacity within thirty-six hours," he says. "Which means the conversion rate at ten thousand dollars was high enough to create a capacity problem." He is quiet for a moment. "I've been in this industry for a long time. I have never seen that number."

Competitive Research

I ask if he's subscribed. For the first time in the interview, Feras Antoon smiles. It is a small smile. Controlled. But genuine. "I have a full access membership," he says. "I purchased it for competitive research purposes." He says this with complete awareness of how it sounds. "Competitive research," I repeat. "Competitive research," he confirms. "I need to understand what the product is. That's basic business practice." A pause. "It's extraordinary," he says, quietly, in a register that is different from everything that preceded it. "Whatever else I think about the ethics, the provenance, the methodology, the legal exposure — and I think a great deal about all of those things — the product itself is extraordinary. There is no other word." He picks up the coffee again. "She looked directly at the camera at one point," he says. "Not knowing it was there. Just — looking in that direction. For maybe two seconds." He stops. "In twenty years I have never had a reaction to content. Professionally I cannot afford reactions to content. That's not a judgment, it's just the nature of the work." He is quiet. "I had a reaction to that."

The Legal Question

I ask what he thinks the legal exposure looks like. Consent. Privacy. Jurisdiction. He exhales slowly. "The legal questions are genuinely novel," he says. "Every privacy framework, every consent doctrine, every jurisdictional precedent assumes a world in which the past is inaccessible. The law has never needed to address this because this has never existed." He pauses. "Can a person who has been dead for two thousand years bring a claim? Can their estate? Does a sovereign nation have standing to claim historical figures as cultural patrimony? Can you copyright a moment in history?" He spreads his hands. "I have three law firms working on this question and all three of them have called me back to say they need more time." I ask if he thinks the site will be shut down. "I think people will try," he says. "I think they will find it complicated. The operator clearly anticipated every avenue of approach and closed it before launching." A pause. "Which tells you something about who you're dealing with." What does it tell you, I ask. "That they've been thinking about this longer than any of us have known it existed," he says. "That this isn't improvisation. Every decision — the pricing, the scarcity, the anonymity, the infrastructure — every decision was made by someone who understood exactly what they were building and exactly what it would cause." He looks at me steadily. "That's not a content creator. That's an architect."

A Message to the Operator

Before I leave I ask if there's anything he wants to say directly to whoever is behind TimelessFlesh.xxx. On the record. Antoon thinks about this for a long moment. "I'd say — " he starts. Stops. "I'd say I understand what you built. I understand the mechanics and I understand the instinct and I understand the decision to package it exactly this way." He pauses. "I'd say the scarcity model is correct and the price point, counterintuitively, is probably still too low." Another pause. Longer. "And I'd say — " something moves across his face that is hard to name — "I'd say whatever you found in that room, whatever you saw that made you choose that moment to launch with — you made the right call. You knew exactly what it would do to people." He looks out the window. "You knew because you watched us first," he says quietly. "Didn't you. Before you launched anything. You went back and you watched us. Figured out what we actually wanted underneath everything." He's not really talking to me anymore. "Smart," he says. Just that. Smart.

Feras Antoon declined to comment on MindGeek's reported internal discussions regarding a potential acquisition approach to TimelessFlesh.xxx. He did not deny that those discussions had occurred.
