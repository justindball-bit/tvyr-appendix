On the Noise

The calls to shut the site down are louder after this release than the first. Governments. Church organizations. Academic bodies. A coalition of privacy advocates who have drafted a letter signed by people whose names I recognize and whose outrage I find genuinely uninteresting. Three separate legal actions in three separate jurisdictions, all of which will resolve the same way the previous ones did — slowly, expensively, and in my favor, because the law was not built for this and the people trying to use it against me know it.

I've been reading the coverage. The arguments against what I do are consistent and I want to log them here because I'm going to address them publicly and I want the private version on record first.

The arguments are: this is an invasion of privacy. This is disrespectful to the dead. This is harmful to the living. This is obscene. This is dangerous. This needs to stop.

Here is what I actually think of those arguments.

Privacy. The word gets used as though it describes a natural right that has always existed and been universally respected. It has not. The entire edifice of recorded history is an invasion of privacy. Every letter that survived its author. Every diary that outlived its writer. Every painting commissioned to show a face that person may not have wanted shown. We have always looked. We have always preserved the looking. I've simply removed the intermediary.

Disrespectful. To whom. Caesar has been a salad dressing punchline for fifty years. Rasputin has been a disco song. The novelty industry in Manhattan has been selling heat-sensitive mugs with Cleopatra on them for three months and nobody is filing legal actions against Rocky Deluca. The selective nature of the outrage is its own argument. They're not upset about disrespect. They're upset about specificity. The footage is too real. That's the problem. Not the disrespect. The reality.

Harmful. This one I take seriously enough to address properly. Harmful to whom. I've read the therapist's open letter. I've read the forum threads. I've read the vox pops and the academic pieces and the church statement. What I see in all of it is not harm. What I see is contact. People making contact with something true about human nature that the modern world has spent considerable energy insulating them from. That contact is uncomfortable. Discomfort is not harm. Discomfort is what happens when the insulation comes off.

The Statement

They want a response. Fine. I'll give them one. I drafted it this morning. It goes on the site tonight.

Public statement — pinned to RawHistory.wtf landing page for 72 hours

For a very long time — and I mean this in the longest possible sense, generations, centuries — the modern world has operated on a specific and unspoken agreement. The agreement is this: we will not look too closely. At what we are. At what we have always been. At what happens in rooms when the doors are closed and the performance stops and the animal underneath the civilization is simply present, doing what it has always done.

We built institutions to enforce this agreement. Laws. Religions. Social codes. Academic frameworks that launder the uncomfortable into the acceptable and call it scholarship. We developed an entire vocabulary of euphemism and redirection and managed distance, and we called that vocabulary civilization, and we enforced it on each other with a consistency that would be impressive if it weren't so exhausting.

And underneath all of it, unchanged, unmodified, completely indifferent to the institutional superstructure built on top of it — humanity. Doing exactly what it has always done. In every century. In every culture. Behind every closed door that the official record chose not to open.

I open the doors.

That is what this site does. That is all it does. It shows you what was actually there, in the rooms, in the moments, without the editorial hand of history deciding what you're allowed to know about yourselves.

Some of what I show is tender. Two people in a palace in Alexandria who, for a few minutes, forgot that one of them was Caesar and one of them was Cleopatra and were simply present with each other. You saw that. Most of you felt something watching it that surprised you. That surprise is the gap between who you've been told you are and who you actually are.

Some of what I show is darker. A man in a St. Petersburg apartment who had resolved questions about God and the body and desire and sin that most people never resolve, and two women who came to him willingly, repeatedly, knowing exactly what they were doing. You saw that too. Some of you are demanding I apologize for it. I'm not going to do that.

History is not a curated exhibition. It is not the pieces your institutions decided you could handle, arranged at a comfortable distance behind protective glass. It is the actual thing — all of it, the tender and the dark and the strange and the ungovernable — and it belongs to you whether you want it or not, because it is what you came from, and pretending otherwise is not dignity.

It is cowardice.

I will continue releasing footage. The archive is deep. What I have found in it is more various, more honest, and more human than anything the official record has been willing to show you. I intend to show you all of it.

You can cancel your subscription.

Most of you won't.
