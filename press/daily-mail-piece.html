'He Was Sent To Us': Meet The People Who Think Rasputin Was A Prophet — And Are Worshipping Him Like One

Inside the strange online movement that RawHistory.wtf accidentally started.
By Caroline Dyce, Feature Correspondent

I want to tell you this started as a funny story. It did start as a funny story. My editor sent me the first TikTok on a Wednesday morning with a single line: page 3, 600 words, have a laugh with it. The TikTok in question showed a young woman in what appeared to be a flat in Nottingham prostrating herself before a printed photograph of Grigori Rasputin that she had taped to her wall between a poster of a K-pop group and what I believe was a motivational quote about manifesting. She was not wearing clothes. She was also, and this is where the funny story starts to develop complications, completely sincere.

"He came to me in a dream," she told her 340,000 followers, having apparently dressed again by the time she got to the explanation portion of the video. "Three nights after I watched the footage. He told me he had been waiting. He told me the footage was always meant to be found. He told me this is the beginning." The beginning of what, she did not specify. Her comment section had 47,000 replies.

By Thursday I had found eleven more accounts. By Friday I had stopped counting.

The Movement

The movement — and I am using that word with full awareness of how much work it is being asked to do — does not have a name, exactly. Online it aggregates under several hashtags, the most prominent of which I will not reproduce here because this is a family publication and also because reproducing it would almost certainly increase its reach and I have complicated feelings about that now that I didn't have on Wednesday morning.

What the accounts share is a specific interpretation of the RawHistory.wtf footage that goes considerably further than anything the academic or theological commentary has suggested. Where Dr. Irina Volkov concluded cautiously that Rasputin appeared to genuinely believe his practices were sanctioned by God, and where the Russian Orthodox Church acknowledged with visible discomfort that the man in the footage was not performing faith, this movement has taken those observations and accelerated them to a conclusion that neither Volkov nor the Patriarchate intended. Rasputin was not a man who believed he was doing God's work. Rasputin was doing God's work. The footage, in this reading, is not a historical document. It is a revelation.

The Voices

I spoke to seven people who hold some version of this belief. They range in age from nineteen to sixty-one. They are from six different countries. None of them know each other personally. All of them found the footage independently and arrived at similar conclusions through what each of them describes, with varying degrees of articulacy, as an experience they did not choose and cannot fully explain.

Melissa, 27, Leeds: "I watched it and something happened to me that I don't have words for. Not like watching something. Like being touched by something. I know how that sounds. I've been saying it to people for two weeks and I know how it sounds and I can't say it differently because that's what happened. He was real. Whatever else he was, he was completely real in a way that most things aren't real. The footage shows that. And if he was that real then the things he believed about himself — I can't just dismiss those. I've tried. I can't."

Tomasz, 34, Kraków: "In Poland we have a complicated relationship with mysticism and the Church and the space between them. I was raised Catholic. I left the Church at twenty. I have not been religious in any formal sense for fourteen years. I watched that footage and I felt something that I last felt at my first communion. I was seven years old. I am thirty-four. I don't know what to do with that."

@devotedtotheholy_one, Instagram, 89,000 followers, identity unknown: Posts daily. Photographs of Rasputin's tomb at the Church of Chesme in St. Petersburg, sent in by followers from around the world. Candles left at the base. Flowers. Notes in multiple languages. In one photograph, submitted last Tuesday, a woman's hands are pressed flat against the stone. In another, submitted Thursday, something that might be a person lying full length against the tomb, arms extended, face down against the cold marble. The account's bio reads: He is not finished.

The Tomb

I should tell you about the tomb. Rasputin was buried at Tsarskoye Selo in 1917. After the Revolution his body was exhumed and burned by a mob and the ashes scattered. There is no actual grave. The tomb at the Church of Chesme contains, as far as anyone knows, nothing. I put this to @devotedtotheholy_one via direct message. The reply came within minutes. That's the point. I stared at that for quite a while.

The pilgrimage element began approximately ten days after the footage dropped. The first documented visit was a twenty-three year old from Berlin named Katerina Wolf, who flew to St. Petersburg specifically to stand at the site of the tomb. "I didn't plan it," she tells me over video call, sitting in what appears to be her bedroom. She is composed, articulate, studying for a masters in comparative literature. She does not look like the person I expected to be talking to. "I booked the flight the same night I watched the footage. Something told me to go. I went." I ask what happened when she got there. "I stood there for about an hour," she says. "I didn't do anything dramatic. I just stood there. But I felt him." She has been back twice since.

What This Actually Is

I want to be careful here about tone because I started this piece with a wink and the wink has been getting progressively harder to sustain and I think honesty requires me to say that directly. These are not, in the main, unwell people. They are not, in the main, people with obvious vulnerabilities being exploited by a charismatic leader. There is no leader. There is no organization. There is no doctrine beyond the shared conviction that the footage showed them something real about a man who was real in a way that most things are not real, and that this realness has implications they are still working out.

What RawHistory.wtf has done — and I suspect this was not part of the business plan, though with this particular operator I am not certain of anything — is create the conditions for a genuine spontaneous religious movement. Leaderless. Decentralized. Built entirely around footage of a dead man in a candlelit room. The Orthodox Church issued a statement calling for restraint and reflection. Katerina Wolf has booked her fourth flight to St. Petersburg.

I asked her, at the end of our conversation, what she thinks Rasputin would make of all this. The pilgrimages, the prostrations, the naked TikToks, the 89,000 followers leaving candles at an empty tomb. She smiled. "He'd think it was completely natural," she said. "He always knew they'd come back."

The Daily Mail requested comment from RawHistory.wtf. No response was received. The site's membership waitlist currently stands at 340,000. @devotedtotheholy_one gained 12,000 followers in the time it took to write this article.
