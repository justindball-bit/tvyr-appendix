Local Teens React To "That History Thing"

We asked. They answered. Eventually.
By Susan Kowalski, Community Correspondent

It took some doing. Getting teenagers to talk to a reporter from the Granville Gazette about TimelessFlesh.xxx required approximately forty minutes of negotiation outside the Dairy Queen on Newark Road, the promise that no last names would be used, and a guarantee — requested with surprising urgency by a boy named Tyler — that the article would not be shared on the Granville High School Facebook group, which Tyler described as "where parents lurk." I agreed to all conditions. What follows is as accurate a transcript as my notepad allowed.

The group is five teenagers. Four boys, one girl. They are sitting on the hood of a car that belongs to none of them — it belongs to an older brother, they explain, who is inside the Dairy Queen and has been informed that his car is being used as a press conference location and has responded with complete indifference. They are: Tyler, 16. Marcus, 17. Jordan, 16. Dex, 17. And Brianna, 16, who is the only one who agreed immediately and without negotiation to speak on the record, and who has been visibly impatient with the boys for the past forty minutes.

Did You Watch It

I ask, to start, if they've seen the footage. There is a silence. The silence has texture. It is the specific silence of five people deciding simultaneously how to answer a question they have absolutely already answered for themselves. "I've heard about it," Tyler says finally. "You watched it four times," Brianna says. "I watched part of it." "You watched it on your phone in second period and Mr. Henderson almost caught you." "That's — " Tyler looks at me. "That's not confirmed." Marcus is looking at the middle distance with the focused expression of a person who has decided that if he doesn't move, the question might not find him. "Marcus," Brianna says. "I'm thinking," Marcus says. "About whether you watched it?" "About how to characterize my media consumption." Dex, who has not yet spoken, says: "We all watched it. Can we just say we all watched it and move on." He says this with the exhausted authority of someone who has been the only honest person in a group for his entire life and has made a kind of peace with it.

Reactions

I ask for their reactions. "It was fine," Tyler says immediately. "It was historically significant," Marcus says, in a tone that suggests he has been practicing this sentence. Jordan, who is the youngest and has been watching the others carefully, says: "It was — " and then stops and looks at his sneakers. "It was a lot," Dex says. To Jordan, not to me. Like he's finishing the sentence. Jordan nods without looking up. "Yeah," he says. "Yeah."

Brianna has been waiting. "Can I just say what I actually thought?" she asks, not really asking. I tell her to go ahead. "I thought it was beautiful," she says. "And I don't mean — " she glances at the boys — "I don't mean like that. I mean the way it was filmed. The light. The way you could tell they actually — " she pauses. "Like they actually liked each other. It wasn't — it didn't feel like what I expected it to feel like." I ask what she expected. "I don't know. Something gross. Something that felt wrong." She picks at the sleeve of her hoodie. "It didn't feel wrong. It felt like watching something really private that you weren't supposed to see but also you couldn't look away because it was — " she stops again. "Because they were just people. Does that make sense?" I tell her it does. Tyler opens his mouth. "Don't," Brianna says, without looking at him. He closes it.

Allergy Season

I ask if any of them talked about it. With each other, with parents, with anyone. The group exchanges a look that contains an entire conversation. "We talked about it," Marcus says carefully. "As a group. Briefly." "For like two hours," Dex says. "It was a short two hours." "Marcus cried," Tyler says. "I didn't — " Marcus turns to me with the urgent expression of someone who needs the record corrected. "I didn't cry. My eyes were doing something. It's allergy season." "It's October," Brianna says. "Late allergy season." "What were you allergic to, Marcus," Dex says. "Julius Caesar?" Marcus is quiet for a moment. "She looked at him," he says finally, quietly, the rehearsed composure completely gone. "Right at the end of the clip before it cuts. She just — looks at him. And it's — " he stops. "It's allergy season," he says again, but he's not really saying it to anyone. Nobody laughs this time.

Jordan

Jordan has been quiet for most of the interview. He is the youngest-looking of the group, slight, with the careful watchfulness of someone who has learned to read rooms. I ask him directly what he thought. He takes a while. "My parents don't really — " he starts. Stops. Starts differently. "They're not like, mean to each other or whatever. They're just kind of — " he moves his hand in a way that means nothing and everything. "Like they're in the same house." Nobody says anything. "And I watched that footage," Jordan says, "and those two people were — even if it was just that night, even if it was just that one thing — they were actually there. With each other." He looks up. "Like actually there. You know?" Dex, who has three older brothers and has clearly heard this particular silence before, puts his hand briefly on Jordan's shoulder and takes it away. "Yeah," Dex says. "Yeah," Marcus says. Tyler says nothing. He is looking at the Dairy Queen sign with great concentration.

Telling Parents

I ask if any of them told their parents they'd watched it. This produces the first genuine laughter of the interview. Unanimous. Loud. The older brother appears briefly in the Dairy Queen window to investigate the noise, sees nothing alarming, disappears again. "My mom would actually die," Tyler says. "She would physically cease to exist." "My dad would try to have a talk with me about it," Marcus says, with a horror that suggests the talk would be considerably worse than the footage. "He did the talk when I watched Titanic. I cannot have the talk about this." "My parents don't know what it is," Dex says. "My dad saw something on the news and asked me if TimelessFlesh was a video game. I said yes. He said it sounded violent. I said it was kind of intense. He said don't play it too much." Jordan smiles for the first time. Small but real. "My mom googled it," he says. The group turns to him. "She didn't say anything to me about it," he says. "But I saw the browser history. She googled it. And then she googled — " he pauses — "she googled 'is TimelessFlesh.xxx real' and then she googled 'Cleopatra and Caesar relationship history' and then she was on that page for like forty minutes." A beat. "My mom," he says. "Everyone's mom," Brianna says gently.

One Question

Before they disperse — the older brother has emerged and wants his car back — I ask one last question. If they could ask whoever is behind TimelessFlesh.xxx one thing, what would it be? Tyler: "How much is he actually making. Ballpark." Marcus: "Is there more of just them. Caesar and Cleopatra specifically. Like other moments." Dex: "How does it work. Like actually. I've been thinking about the physics of it and I can't — how does it work." Brianna thinks about it seriously. "I'd ask him if he knew," she says. "When he was editing it. If he knew it was going to make people feel like that. Or if it surprised him too." She looks at me. "I think he knew," she says. "I think he knew exactly what he was doing. But I think maybe it still surprised him a little bit. When he watched it back." She pauses. "I think he felt it too. Even him."

Jordan is last. He thinks for a long time. Long enough that the older brother, who has been jangling his keys with escalating impatience, actually stops jangling. "I'd ask him," Jordan says slowly, "if he ever watches things that aren't — " he searches — "that aren't the exciting stuff. Like not the battles or the scandals. Just — people. Regular people. Just living." I ask why. "Because I think that's the real thing," he says. "I think the other stuff is what he sells. But I think late at night he watches the regular stuff." He shrugs, small and certain. "I think that's where he actually goes."

The older brother's name is Connor. He did not wish to comment for this article. He has, he confirmed when pressed, also seen the footage. "It's whatever," he said. He said it the way people say things they don't mean at all.
