"She Chose That Ground": Mary Shelley, Percy Shelley, and the Footage That Changes Everything

A biographer of twenty-five years reckons with what she did not know.
By Camellia Margaret

I have been writing about Mary Shelley for twenty-five years. I want to say that at the outset because I think it matters. Not as a credential — credentials are beside the point now — but as context for what I am about to tell you. Twenty-five years. Every letter she wrote and every letter written to her. Every diary entry, including the ones she later crossed out. Every account of people who knew her, liked her, resented her, misunderstood her, which was most of them. I have read the grocery lists. I have read the marginalia. I have stood in the rooms she lived in and tried to hear something in the walls. I believed, going into last Tuesday, that I knew Mary Shelley as well as it is possible to know someone you have never met and who has been dead for a hundred and seventy years. I watched the footage on Wednesday morning. I am going to need to revise that belief.

What We Knew

The St Pancras visits are documented. Mary's own journals record them — references to afternoons spent at her mother's grave, reading, thinking, meeting Percy. The relationship between Mary Godwin and Percy Bysshe Shelley began in that churchyard in the spring of 1814 and developed there across weeks of increasingly charged encounters. What scholars have always known, and what the journals confirm, is that the churchyard was central to their courtship in a way that went beyond convenience or privacy.

Mary Wollstonecraft — Mary's mother, dead of childbed fever ten days after her daughter's birth — was buried at St Pancras. Mary had been visiting that grave since childhood. It was where she went to think. Where she went to feel connected to the woman she never knew but whose work had shaped her entirely. The Vindication of the Rights of Woman. The woman who argued that female intellect was not inferior to male intellect and that society's insistence otherwise was its own indictment. Her daughter grew up in the shadow of that argument and in the shadow of that grave and when Percy Shelley — brilliant, reckless, already married, already a father — when he came into her life, it was to that grave she brought him.

Scholars have long understood this as symbolically significant. Mary choosing to share her most private space with the man she was falling in love with. Mary conducting her courtship on the ground that connected her to her dead mother. What scholars did not know — what I did not know, what no one knew — was what happened on that ground. The footage answers that question.

What The Footage Shows

I am going to be precise here because I think precision is what the moment requires. Percy arrives first. He is twenty-one years old and he moves through the churchyard with the specific ease of someone who has been there before and is comfortable there. He is not looking around. He is not nervous. He stands at the grave and waits and there is something in the waiting — the quality of it, the stillness — that I recognised immediately. I have read his letters. I know how he waited for things he wanted.

Mary arrives perhaps two minutes into the footage. She is eighteen years old. I have looked at every portrait of Mary Shelley. The ones painted from life and the ones painted from memory and the ones painted from imagination. I thought I knew her face. I did not know her face. The portraits give you the architecture. They do not give you the weather. What the footage gave me, in the first ten seconds of seeing her cross the churchyard toward Percy, was the weather of a face I have spent twenty-five years trying to reconstruct from documents.

She was not what I expected. I want to be careful about what I mean by that. She was not softer than I expected, or more vulnerable, or any of the things that people assume when they learn a young woman is about to do something transgressive in a churchyard with a married man. She was not being led. She was not hesitating. She moved toward him with the specific directness of someone who has already made a decision and is now simply enacting it.

Percy

I have never been kind to Percy Shelley in my work. This is not a secret. My biography is frequently cited as one of the more critical assessments of his treatment of Mary — the absences, the other women, the way his genius consumed everything around it including her. I stand by that assessment. The footage does not change it. What the footage does is complicate it in a way I was not prepared for. He is present with her in that churchyard in a way that his letters sometimes suggested and his actions often contradicted. The quality of his attention in those minutes is complete. She has it entirely. Whatever else he was, whatever he did before and after, in that churchyard on that night he is entirely there. I found that harder to sit with than I expected. I have spent twenty-five years being appropriately critical of Percy Bysshe Shelley. The footage did not make me uncritical. But it made the criticism more complicated. Which is, I suppose, what the truth usually does.

The Location

I need to say something about the grave specifically because I think it has been underaddressed in the coverage I have read since the footage dropped. Mary chose her mother's grave. Not a convenient location. Not a private location she happened to know. The grave of the woman whose death caused her own birth, whose ideas shaped her entire intellectual formation, whose absence defined her childhood. She brought Percy there and she chose that ground and the footage makes it entirely clear that the choice was deliberate.

I have two reactions to this and I am going to record both of them because I think intellectual honesty requires it. The first reaction is professional. It reframes the entire early relationship in a way that shifts Mary from subject to agent. She was not a young woman swept up by a charismatic older man. She was a young woman who made a series of deliberate choices, including this one, and the radicalism of those choices was visible from the beginning to anyone who was paying attention. The second reaction is personal and harder to articulate. I have visited that grave. I have stood on that ground on a grey London afternoon and tried to feel something of what Mary felt there. I thought I had some access to it through twenty-five years of documents and proximity and careful attention. I did not have access to it. The footage has access to it. And watching it I felt something I have not felt in a long time in relation to this work — the specific humility of being shown that the thing you have spent your life studying is larger than your study of it.

Mary Shelley was larger than my study of her. The footage knows that.

Frankenstein was published two years later. I am not going to make that argument explicitly. I don't need to. Anyone who has read the novel and watched the footage will make it themselves.

Camellia Margaret is the author of several works on the Romantic period. She has been writing about Mary Shelley for twenty-five years. She is currently revising a manuscript she thought was finished. She did not respond to a request for further comment. She was, according to her assistant, at St Pancras Old Church.
