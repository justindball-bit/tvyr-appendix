An Open Letter to the Operators of RawHistory.wtf

A clinical psychologist on what nineteen years of practice did not prepare her for.
By Dr. Patricia Henley, PhD — Licensed Clinical Psychologist, Edinburgh

I want to be clear about what this letter is and what it is not. It is not a call for censorship. I have read the site operator's statement and while I find his contempt for institutional concern somewhat easier to dismiss than he apparently expects, I am not naive enough to believe that legal suppression of this material is either achievable or, in any uncomplicated sense, desirable. The letter is not that. It is not a moral condemnation. I am a psychologist, not a priest. The ethical questions raised by RawHistory.wtf are genuine and significant and I will leave them to the philosophers and theologians who are better equipped to address them. Several have. Their work is worth reading.

What this letter is, is a clinical observation. Submitted publicly because I believe the public record should contain it, because I believe the people running this site should be aware of it, and because frankly I have not known what else to do with what I have seen in my consulting room over the past two weeks and writing it down is the closest thing to processing it that I have available to me.

What I Have Seen

I have been in practice for nineteen years. I work primarily with adults presenting with anxiety, relational difficulties, and what I would broadly describe as problems of meaning — the specific modern difficulty of living in a world that provides enormous amounts of stimulation and very little framework for integrating it. In the two weeks following Release Two of RawHistory.wtf, I have seen eleven patients who referenced the footage either directly or obliquely in session. Eleven out of a patient list of forty-three. In two weeks.

I want to be careful about how I characterize what they brought to me, because clinical material is not mine to share and because the presentations were varied enough that a simple summary would misrepresent them. What I can say, in general terms, is this: the material is not producing what I would characterize as trauma responses. It is not producing disgust, or straightforward distress, or the kind of intrusive re-experiencing associated with genuinely harmful content. What it is producing is harder to name and in some ways more difficult to work with clinically.

The closest I can get is this: the footage appears to be creating a specific kind of dissonance in the people who watch it. A gap between what they expected to feel and what they actually felt. And that gap — that specific distance between the anticipated response and the actual response — is where they are getting stuck. Several patients described watching the footage expecting to feel one thing and feeling something else. Something less simple. Something that implicated them in ways they weren't prepared for. One patient, a woman in her late forties who I will describe no further, used the phrase recognized by it. She watched the footage and felt recognized by it. Not seen in the way of surveillance. Recognized in the way of being accurately identified. She found this deeply unsettling. She also, she told me, watched it a second time.

On the Operator's Argument

The site operator's public statement argues that what his footage produces is not harm but contact. That the discomfort people feel is the discomfort of insulation being removed. That this is a feature rather than a defect. I have been thinking about that argument for several days. Clinically, I cannot entirely dismiss it. The dissonance I am describing in my patients is not, strictly speaking, damage. It is more like the specific discomfort of a person who has been shown something true and is in the process of integrating it. That process is uncomfortable. It is also, in my professional experience, sometimes necessary. I have spent nineteen years helping people close the gap between who they present themselves as and who they actually are. It is slow work. It is often painful. It is, when it succeeds, valuable.

I am not prepared to say that what RawHistory.wtf is doing is the same thing. I am not prepared to say it is entirely different either. What I am prepared to say — and this is the sentence this letter has been building toward, the one I have rewritten more times than I can count, the one I am going to leave in because intellectual honesty requires it — is that the footage appears to be doing something real to the people who watch it. Not fake real. Not performed real. Actually real, in the way that very few things in the modern cultural landscape are actually real, and that this realness is both the source of whatever value it has and the source of whatever harm it does, and that I do not yet know how to separate those two things cleanly.

I have referred two patients to colleagues. I have extended session time for three others. I have sat with the remaining six and done what I was trained to do, which is hold the space while they work toward something they can live with.

Full Disclosure

I am also, in the interests of full disclosure, and because I believe the public record should contain this too — I watched it myself. I watched Release One in October, as most people did, with a mixture of professional curiosity and the same involuntary response that Dr. Caroline Marsh described in her National Geographic memorandum. I watched Release Two last Thursday evening after my last patient of the day, alone in my consulting room, with what I told myself was clinical intent. I sat with it for a long time afterward. I am not going to tell you what I felt. That is mine. But I will tell you that I understand, in a way I did not before Thursday evening, why eleven of my forty-three patients brought it into the room with them.

It gets in. That is the clinical observation I am submitting for the public record. It gets in, and it does not come out cleanly, and I do not know yet whether that is the most important thing about it or simply the most inconvenient.

Dr. Patricia Henley is a licensed clinical psychologist in private practice in Edinburgh, Scotland. She has nineteen years of clinical experience specialising in adult anxiety and relational difficulties. She will not be commenting further on this letter or on RawHistory.wtf.

She has not cancelled her subscription.
