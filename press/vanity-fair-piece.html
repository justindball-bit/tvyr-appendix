Vanity Fair

"They Was Always Doing That"

Ol' Dirty Bastard has thoughts on history, privacy, Cleopatra, and why none of this surprises him even a little bit.
By Jessica Hoffmann

The club is called Dirty's. Of course it is. It is eleven in the morning and Ol' Dirty Bastard — born Russell Tyrone Jones, known to the world by a name that was always more philosophy than nickname — is already here, already comfortable, occupying a booth in the back corner with the settled authority of a man who is exactly where he intends to be. The chairs are still upturned on the tables. The cleaning crew is working around him with the practiced ease of people who have learned not to make eye contact before noon. He is drinking something from a large styrofoam cup. He does not explain what it is. He offers me some. I decline. He shrugs, unbothered, and leans back. I have been told I have forty-five minutes. We go for two and a half hours.

I ask him about TimelessFlesh.xxx. He nods slowly, the way a man nods when someone has finally brought up the thing he's been waiting to talk about. "Julius Caesar," he says. Not as a question. As a statement of fact that contains, somehow, an entire opinion. I ask what he thought when he saw the footage. "I thought — " he pauses, takes a pull from the cup — "I thought, yeah. Obviously. You know what I mean? Obviously that's what was happening. You think those people were just sitting around talking about the Senate?" He gestures broadly at the club around him, as if the club itself is evidence. "People always been doing exactly this. Since the beginning. That's not a revelation. That's just Tuesday."

He has, it emerges, watched the clip many times. How many times, I ask. He thinks about this with genuine seriousness. "Enough times that I understand it," he says finally. "There's a number where you stop watching it like it's a thing and you start watching it like it's people. I watched it that many times." I ask what he means. "She's running that whole situation," he says. "The whole time. And he knows it and she knows it and that's — " he points at me — "that's the actual history right there. That's what they don't put in the books. The books say Julius Caesar conquered Egypt. I watched that footage and I'm like, did he though?" He laughs. It is a large laugh, full of genuine delight. "Did he though."

On Authenticity

The question of authenticity has consumed the academic world for seventy-two hours. I ask ODB if he has any doubts about whether the footage is real. He looks at me for a moment. "You ever just know something?" he says. "Like not because somebody told you and not because you read it somewhere. Just because your body knows it the way your body knows it's cold outside before you open the door?" I say yes. "That footage is real," he says simply. "My body told me in the first ten seconds. Wasn't nothing made by a computer ever gonna make me feel like that. Computers don't know about — " he pauses, searching — "about the way people are with each other when nobody's watching. Computers don't know about that yet." He takes another pull from the cup. "Whoever's got this ability," he says, "I respect it. I deeply respect it. But I got questions."

The Questions

What questions, I ask. He leans forward. This is clearly the part he's been building toward. "Okay so. He can look back at any moment in history. Any moment. And he chose this one to launch with." He spreads his hands. "Why this one? He could've started with the pyramids being built. He could've started with whatever. He started with this." Another gesture at the club. "Which means he knows. He knows what people actually want, right, underneath all the — " he waves a hand at the concept of civilisation generally — "underneath all of that. And he knows it's this. It was always this." He sits back. "I knew that too," he says, without any particular ego. Just as a fact. "That's why I opened the club."

I ask if he's subscribed. "Full access," he says immediately. "Ten thousand. I didn't even think about it. That's not even a question." I ask if he thinks five hundred dollars a month is too much. He looks genuinely confused by the question. "For that?" he says. "Five hundred dollars a month is cheap. What I would pay to watch history like that —" he shakes his head. "There's no number. You understand what I'm saying? There's no number on that. Whoever set the price underpriced it." He pauses. "Don't tell them I said that."

What He Wants Next

I ask what he wants to see next. He doesn't hesitate for even a fraction of a second. "Nefertiti," he says. I ask why. "Because everybody talks about Cleopatra but Nefertiti was — " he stops. Looks at the ceiling. Seems to be trying to locate the right words in the right order. "There's a bust of her, right, in a museum in Berlin. You seen it?" I have seen it. "Okay so you know," he says, as if that settles it. "You know there's a whole story in that face that nobody has ever told. I want to know what that story is. I want to see who she actually was when it was just her." He pauses. "And whoever was with her." He grins. It is, in its own way, exactly Caligula's grin. The full uncomplicated appetite of a man who knows what he wants and sees no reason to apologize for wanting it. "I'd pay ten thousand dollars just for that one," he says. "Tell them I said that too. I don't care."

Showing Receipts

As I'm leaving, I ask if there's anything else he wants to say about it. He thinks for a moment. A cleaning woman moves past with a mop. Music starts somewhere, quiet, barely audible. "People act surprised," he says finally. "That's what I don't understand. People are acting like this is some crazy revelation about human nature." He shakes his head slowly. "This is not a revelation about human nature. This is a confirmation. This is somebody finally showing receipts." He looks out at the empty club, the upturned chairs, the morning light coming through the tinted windows at a low angle, turning everything amber. "We was always like this," he says. "Since the beginning. Caesar and Cleopatra. All of them. Just like this." A pause. "I find that beautiful, personally."

Ol' Dirty Bastard's club, Dirty's, is located in Brooklyn, New York. This interview has been condensed and edited. He still has the styrofoam cup.
